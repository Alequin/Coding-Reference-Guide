Software Prototype Model

Prototyping to the production of an application which displays a systems functionality but does not hold the actual logic. It enables the acquisition of valuable feedback at early stages of development.

The main purpose of prototypes is to allow customers and users to evaluate developer proposals prior to implementations and to gain a better understanding of requirements via feedback.

Phases of Prototyping

  • Basic Requirement Identification: This involves understanding the very basic product requirements in terms of user interface. Internal design, performance and security may be ignored at this stage.
  • Developing the Initial Prototype: The initial prototype is developed, which showcases the basic requirements and user interface. Again the features may not work in the same way as the actual software and act as a way to provide the customer a way to view the direct the product is taking.
  • Review of the Prototype: The developed prototype is presented to the customer and feedback is collected.
  • Revise and Enhance Prototype: In this phase the feedback is discussed and negotiations take place with the customer.

When to use

  • When the desired system needs to have lots of interaction with the end users (online systems, web interfaces ect...).

    This ensures constant feedback, which will be incorporated into each prototype.

    Advantages

    • Users are actively involved in the development
    • Errors are detected easily
    • Quick and useful feedback
    • Able to identify difficult or confusing functionality easily

    Disadvantages

    • Leads to implementing and the repairing way of building systems
    • May increase complexity beyond scope based on feedback and customer desires
    • Incomplete applications may result in misuse an incorrect use of the system during user testing
    • Possibility of poor requirement analysis

    Rapid Application Development

    The RAD Model is based on prototyping and iterative development with little planning, as planning is involved with writing the software itself. It focuses on rapid production of prototypes, customer testing of prototypes, gathering requirements via workshops or focus groups, reuse of prototypes, continuous integration and rapid development.

    RAD makes use of iterative and incremental methods to produce functional prototypes of modules and the system. A key requirement of this model is that prototypes are resuable.

    Agile Model

    This model is a combination of iterative and incremental process models with focus on process adaptability and customer satisfaction by rapid delivery. Agile methods break the product into small incremental builds, which take place over one to three weeks and involve stages of the SDLC such as planning, analysis, design, development and testing.

    The four key principles of agile development include...

    • Individuals and Interactions: Self organisation is important by so are interactions such as pair programming
    • Working Software: Working demos are the best means of customer communication
    • Customer Collaboration: Continuous customer interaction is important to enable the maintenance and development of requirements
    • Responding to Change: Agile is focused on quick response to change and continuous developments

    Agile is based on adaptive software development methods, rather than predictive methods. Predictive methods require detailed and strict planning and a forecast of development over the next few months.

    There is no detailed planning in adaptive methods. Development is feature driven and teams adapt to the changing product dynamically. Testing occurs frequently to reduce risk.

    When to use

    • When Changes to product are common. Frequency of increments makes implementing changes cheap as low risk

    Advantages

    • Promotes team work
    • Rapid development of easily demonstrated functionality
    • Low resource requirements
    • Easy to change requirements
    • Minimal rules, documentation easily employed
    • Short planning stages
    • Easy to manage
    • Gives flexibility to developers

    Disadvantages

    • Not suitable for complex dependencies
    • Risky in regards to sustainability and maintainability
    • Will not work without an overall plan, agile leader and agile project manager practice
    • Depends on good customer interactions. Unclear communication may lead teams in the wrong direction
    • Challenging to include new team members due to limited documentation

    Spiral Model

    The spiral model is a combination between iterative development and the controlled aspects of the waterfall model. It has four phases...

    • Identification: The basic requirements are gathered in the baseline spiral.

      In subsequent spirals identification of further requirements, subsystem requirements and unit requirements are considered. Customer communication is also done in this phase to consider their opinion of the current software.
    • Design: In the baseline spiral the conceptual design is produced.

      In subsequent spirals the architecture, modules and front end design are further considered and enhanced.
    • Construct / Build: Here the product undergoes production. In the initial spiral a proof of concept (POC) version is produced, to enable feedback.

      In subsequent spirals a working model of the system is produced (called build), each with a version number.
    • Evaluation / Risk Analysis: Here risk analysis is conducted to identify, estimate and monitor the technical feasibility, management risks, schedule issues and cost issues.

    When to use

    • When budget is limited and risk evaluation is important
    • For medium to high risk projects
    • Unsure of full requirements
    • Requirements are complex and need clarity
    • A lot of customer feedback is required
    • Changes are expected during the development process

    Advantages

    • Changing requirements can be accommodated
    • Allows extensive use of prototypes
    • Users see system early
    • Development can be divided into smaller parts

    Disadvantages

    • Management is more complex
    • Not suitable for small / low risk projects
    • expensive for small projects
    • Complex process
    • Spiral may go indefinitely
    • Large number of stages requires excessive documentation

    Iterative Model

    In the Iterative model a small set of software requirements are identified, implemented and tested. The product is then reviewed, further requirements are identified, development begins again and tests are repeated and extended. This process is repeated with a new version of the software being produced and the end of each iteration.

    The basic idea behind this model is to produce a product through repeated cycles within small portions of time. It is a combination of iterative design and incremental building.

    Stages

    • Planning and Requirements
    • Analysis and Design
    • Implementation
    • Testing
    • Evaluation
    • Repeat

    When to use

    • Requirements of the complete system are clearly understood
    • The project is large and can be broken into smaller increments
    • At least the major requirements are understood and defined (some functionalities may evolve or be decided on later)
    • Useful when there is a to market constraint
    • A new technology is being used and learnt by the development team
    • Specific skills are required but not available and will contracted for specific iterations
    • There are high risk features and goals may be changed

    Advantages

    • A working model of the system is available during early development. This makes identifying issues early easier.
    • Operational product is available with each increment
    • Able to present incomplete iterations of product to customer for review and feedback
    • Some functionality can be developed quickly and early in the life cycle.
    • Versioning of a system is easy
    • Parallel development is possible
    • Progress can be measured
    • Cheap to change minor details in scope or requirements
    • Testing and debugging during small iterations is easy
    • Easy to manage risk

    Disadvantages

    • May require more resources
    • While changing requirements is cheap it is not suited to large changes
    • Possible design issues as not all requirements are decided on at the beginning of the life cycle
    • Not suitable for small projects
    • More Management attention is required
    • End of project may not be known
    • Project progress is dependent on analysis phase
    • Highly skilled resources are required for risk analysis
    • Promotes feature creep and repeated requests for further development

    Waterfall Model

    This model, also known as the linear-sequential life cycle model, was the first process to be used. It this model each stage cascades into the next and does not take place until the previous is complete.

    • Requirement Analysis: System requirements are decided on and documented
    • System Design: The requirements are studied and the system design is prepared using them as a guide. The design phase defines the software architecture to be used and the hardware requirements.
    • Implementation: Using the design the system is developed in small programs called units. Each unit us developed and tested, which is known as unit testing.
    • Testing and Integration: The units are integrated into a system and the result is tested for faults and failures.
    • Deployment: Once testing is done the product is released.
    • Maintenance: Once released the product may be patched or enhanced.

    When to use

    • Requirements are very well documented, clear and fixed.
    • Product definition is stable.
    • Technology is understood and is not dynamic.
    • There are no ambiguous requirements.
    • Ample resources with required expertise are available to support the product.
    • The project is short.

    Advantages

    • Easy to schedule deadlines for stages
    • Easy to use and understand
    • Easy to manage
    • Works well for small projects with well defined requirements
    • Easy to arrange tasks
    • Easy to produce technical documentation
    • Easy to estimate production costs

    Disadvantages

    • Difficult to re-engineer if results are not as expected
    • Little opportunity for reflection and revision
    • No production until the late stages of the life cycle
    • High risk
    • Poor model for complex and OOP projects
    • Poor model for long projects
    • Little opportunity to update or change requirments

    What is the Software Development Life Cycle

    The Software Development Life Cycle (sdlc) or software development process describes the distinct work phases and methods used to plan, design, develop and test software.

    The sdlc is a process used by those who create software to plan, develop maintain, replace, alter and enhance software. Common stages of the sdlc include...

    • Planning: Planning (or requirement analysis) is the most important stage. Input from multiple sources is used to outline the requirements of the desired system, required resources, costs and overall production time. Plans should also consider the quality assurance requirements and identify possible risks.
    • Defining: Projects requirements must be clearly defined and documented in a software requirement specification (SRS) document.
    • Analysis and Design: Using the SRS high level designs of the system are produced. The overall architecture is planned and the technologies to be used are selected. Time constraints and cost are reviewed and the best design approach is selected.

      The architectural design defines all the components that need to be developed, use of third party services, database connections, front end design and behaviour and system use cases.

      More than one approach may be proposed and documented in a Designing Document Specification (DDS).
    • Development: In this stage the product is built. This phase marks the start of production
    • Testing: The product is tested for defects and bugs and fixed until it reaches the desired quality. This stage is commonly a subset within other stages in most modern sdlc models.
    • Deployment: Once complete the product is released.
